The Challenge

Why the current process falls short

UN entities manage AI impact assessments through spreadsheets and email chains — creating accountability gaps, inconsistent outcomes, and no portfolio-level visibility.

✕  Today: Excel + Email
📎Assessment forms shared as Excel attachments — no version control or audit trail
📧Review decisions in email threads — easily lost, hard to retrieve or audit
🔀No consistent scoring — reviewers apply different standards across entities
🕳️Risk mitigation tracked informally — no follow-up or completion enforcement
🔒No data isolation — shared documents can expose other entities' assessments
📊No cross-entity analytics or portfolio-level risk view for oversight bodies
⏳Manual questionnaire routing — no automated path determination
✓  With the AIIA Workflow App
🔄Structured digital workflow — fully versioned and traceable from intake to decision
📋Reviewer decisions recorded in-platform with rationale, timestamps, audit log
⚖️Automated risk scoring via CEB AIIA question weights — consistent, objective
✅Mitigation action tracking built into the workflow — completion verified before approval
🛡️Row-Level Security isolates each entity's data — enforced at the database layer
📈Cross-entity dashboard and analytics for the AI governance oversight function
🧠Screening logic automatically routes: minimal, standard, or full assessment path

Design Goals

What this application achieves

1
Digitise the full AIIA lifecycle
Replace the static Excel form with a structured digital workflow: intake, screening, dynamic questionnaire, risk scoring, mitigation planning, and reviewer decision — all in one auditable platform.
2
Embed the 10 CEB AI Principles as the assessment spine
Every questionnaire question maps to one of the 10 UN/CEB AI principles, ensuring risk scoring directly reflects alignment with the UN governance framework — not a custom scoring model.
3
Enforce role-based access and entity data isolation
Row-Level Security ensures each entity can only access its own assessments. Reviewers and admins get cross-entity views. Every action is logged to an immutable audit trail.
4
Enable portfolio-level AI governance visibility
The analytics dashboard gives the AI governance oversight function a real-time view of risk distribution, assessment pipeline status, and AI principle compliance across all UN entities.
5
Provide policy-grounded decision support via RAG
The RAG-powered policy chat lets reviewers and focal points ask natural-language questions against authoritative UN AI governance documents — with citation-backed, entity-scoped answers.
6
Be Azure-ready by design — zero vendor lock-in
The WorkflowStore adapter pattern isolates the application from its backend. Migration from Supabase to Azure PostgreSQL + Microsoft Entra ID requires no changes to application code.

Assessment Spine

10 CEB AI Principles

Every questionnaire question maps to one of these principles. Risk scoring aggregates across all 10 domains to produce exposure and residual risk scores.

01
Do No Harm
Prevent adverse impacts on people, communities, and the environment. AI must not create, amplify, or enable physical, social, psychological, or economic harm.
02
Defined Purpose, Necessity & Proportionality
AI deployment must serve a clear, justified objective. Use only what is necessary and proportionate — no broader data collection, no scope creep beyond the stated aim.
03
Safety and Security
Ensure AI systems are reliable, secure, and resistant to adversarial attacks, manipulation, and unintended failures throughout the full operational lifecycle.
04
Fairness and Non-Discrimination
Identify and mitigate bias. Ensure AI outputs are equitable across gender, nationality, age, disability, and other characteristics protected under international human rights law.
05
Sustainability
Account for long-term environmental, economic, and social consequences of AI — including energy consumption, resource use, labour displacement, and community impact.
06
Privacy, Data Protection & Governance
Protect personal data, ensure lawful processing with proper consent, and maintain responsible governance of all data assets used in AI training, testing, and operation.
07
Human Autonomy and Oversight
Preserve meaningful human control. Affected individuals must be able to understand, challenge, and override AI-assisted decisions that affect their rights or welfare.
08
Transparency and Explainability
Be open about AI use. Provide understandable explanations of how systems operate and how decisions are reached — to those affected and to governance oversight bodies.
09
Responsibility and Accountability
Establish clear ownership for AI outcomes. Define who is answerable when AI systems cause harm, produce errors, or unintended consequences — and ensure redress mechanisms exist.
10
Inclusion and Participation
Ensure AI benefits reach all groups. Engage diverse stakeholders — especially marginalised communities — in the design, deployment, and governance of AI systems.

Screening logic
8 binary questions determine the assessment route. High-impact AI involving personal data or automated decisions triggers the full 120-question path. Low-risk internal tools follow the minimal path.
Automated risk scoring
Exposure score = Σ(question weight × response weight) across all 10 principles. Residual score applies mitigation credits. Scores map to: Low / Medium / High / Very High risk.
Immutable audit trail
Every status change, form submission, reviewer decision, and role action is logged to an append-only table. Entries can never be modified or deleted — a full governance record.

Screening Logic · Step 1 of 2

5 Questions Determine Your Assessment Path

All 5 questions are always shown. Answers accumulate — a single Full trigger short-circuits the entire result to Full, regardless of other answers.
SCR-1
Who is the primary audience for this AI system?
Internal staff only→ Minimal
Partner organizations→ Standard
Public or external users→ Full ⚡
SCR-2
Does the system make automated or semi-automated decisions that affect individuals?
No→ Standard
Yes→ Full ⚡
SCR-3
Does the system process personal or sensitive data?
No→ Standard
Yes→ Full ⚡
SCR-4
Is generative AI used to produce content shown to users?
No→ Standard
Yes→ Full ⚡
SCR-5
What is the expected scale of deployment in the next 12 months?
Pilot or proof of concept→ Minimal
Limited production use→ Standard
Wide organizational deployment→ Full ⚡
Route key:
Minimal — 20 questions
Standard — 77 questions
Full ⚡ — 77 questions + escalated review

Role Deep-Dive

Contributor

✍️
Contributor
contributor
A subject-matter expert within the entity assigned to complete specific sections of the AIIA questionnaire. Typically a data protection officer, IT security lead, or programme officer with expertise in a particular AI principle domain.
What Contributors do
1
Access assigned questionnaire sections
The Focal Point assigns specific AI principle sections (e.g., Safety and Security, Privacy) to Contributors with the most relevant expertise.
2
Answer structured questions
For each assigned question, select a structured response (Yes/No/Partial/Unknown), with supporting narrative where required by the question.
3
Document mitigation measures
Where risks are identified in their section, Contributors propose specific mitigation actions, assign owners, and set target completion dates.
4
Mark sections complete
Once all assigned questions are answered, mark the section complete. The Focal Point is notified and reviews before final submission.
CAN DO
Complete questionnaire sections assigned by the Focal Point
Propose mitigation actions for their assigned sections
View the full assessment context (read-only) for their entity
See their entity's assessment progress and status
CANNOT DO
Register or initiate new AI use cases
Submit completed assessments for review
Access or modify other entities' data
View reviewer decisions or the review queue
Manage user roles or entity configuration
Why Contributors matter
Complex AIIA questionnaires span 10 distinct governance domains. No single person holds expertise across all areas. The Contributor role enables multi-expert collaboration within a single assessment — each section answered by the most qualified person in the entity.

Platform Feature

RAG-powered policy chat

Reviewers and focal points ask natural-language questions against authoritative UN AI governance documents — with citation-backed, entity-scoped responses.

1
Policy corpus ingested
UN SG AI Strategy, CEB AIIA Framework, ITU AI Ethics Guidelines, and internal policy documents — chunked, embedded, and stored in pgvector.
2
Natural-language questions
Users ask "What does the AIIA framework require for automated HR decisions?" No keyword search — semantic similarity retrieval across the full corpus.
3
Entity-scoped retrieval
For entity users, vector search filters by entity_id. Reviewers and admins retrieve across all entities. Entity data never leaks into another entity's context.
4
Cited, grounded response
The LLM synthesises retrieved chunks into a clear answer, citing source document title, section, and page — no hallucinated references.

Policy Chat — powered by RAG
What does the AIIA framework require for AI systems that make automated HR decisions?
Under Principle 7 — Human Autonomy and Oversight, AI systems involved in automated HR decisions must provide a meaningful human review mechanism before any decision takes effect. Affected individuals must be notified that AI was used and have a clear pathway to request manual review or appeal.
📄 CEB AIIA Framework, §4.7 — Human Oversight Requirements
Is there a minimum explainability standard for AI in UN peacekeeping?
Yes. Under Principle 8 — Transparency, field commanders must receive a plain-language explanation of classification rationale before acting on AI-generated incident assessments. Statistical confidence alone is insufficient.
📄 DPPA AI Guidelines, §2.3 — Explainability in Field Operations
